# Recovery And Fatigue

## Sources
- American College of Sports Medicine. Progression models in resistance training for healthy adults. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19204579/
- Schoenfeld BJ et al. Resistance training volume enhances muscle hypertrophy but not strength in trained men. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 2019. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30153194/

## Practical Principles
- More work is useful only when the athlete adapts to it. The athlete's recent recovery and adherence change the prescription.
- Technical sports and calisthenics skills degrade when fatigue is too high; quality needs protection.
- High volume can support hypertrophy, but it can also reduce skill quality if placed before priority work.

## Programming Rules
- If energy or recovery is low, reduce hard set count, density, or leverage difficulty before removing the technical objective.
- Separate the hardest skill exposure from the most fatiguing accessory work when possible.
- Use deload or consolidation weeks after repeated fatigue signals, stalled progress, or pain escalation.
- Keep at least one session per week that reinforces the main skill without maximal fatigue.

## Use In SessionAI
- The plan must adapt to `energy`, `recovery`, and `adherence` fields.
- The program should not chase novelty when the athlete needs continuity and measurable progression.
