# Resistance Training Principles

## Sources
- American College of Sports Medicine. Progression models in resistance training for healthy adults. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 2009. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19204579/
- Schoenfeld BJ, Grgic J, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. Strength and hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs high-load resistance training: systematic review and meta-analysis. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 2017. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28834797/
- Schoenfeld BJ, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. Effects of resistance training frequency on measures of muscle hypertrophy: systematic review and meta-analysis. Sports Medicine, 2016. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27102172/

## Practical Principles
- Progression should match training status, target goal, recovery capacity, and technical stability.
- Strength-biased work generally needs high intent, sufficiently heavy or hard variations, longer rests, and clear exposure to the target movement pattern.
- Hypertrophy can be produced across a wide loading range when sets are sufficiently challenging, but strength expression is more specific to heavier/harder work.
- Weekly volume matters, but only while the athlete can recover and preserve execution quality.
- Frequency is a distribution tool: spreading volume over multiple exposures can improve quality and tolerance.

## Programming Rules
- Increase only one or two stressors at a time: load, leverage, reps, sets, density, range, or complexity.
- Keep 1-3 reps in reserve or an equivalent technical margin on most working sets unless the coach explicitly wants a test.
- Use longer rest for key strength/skill sets: usually 120-240 seconds.
- Use shorter rest for accessory hypertrophy or trunk work: usually 60-120 seconds.
- If feedback shows low energy, poor recovery, pain, or falling adherence, reduce total hard sets before increasing intensity.

## Use In SessionAI
- Every generated program must define the main progression variable.
- The plan must explain why volume, frequency, and exercise difficulty fit the athlete history.
- Avoid generic overload language: specify exactly what changes next week if performance is good.
